Chem 260: Thermodynamics, Equilibria, and Kinetics (Spring 2019)
Class Schedule

The schedule below outlines each day's main topic(s) along with reading assignments and suggested end-of-chapter problems from the textbook, and additional essays that cover topics not included in the textbook. A brief quiz, which is one of the suggested problems from the textbook, is given at the end of class on those days shown in bold italics; unit exams are shown in bold.

Unit I: Thermodynamics

In which we develop an understanding of why some reactions are favorable and other reactions are unfavorable, and learn how to predict if a reaction is favorable and how that favorability is affected by temperature and the composition of the reaction mixture.

  • January 28: introduction to course (skim Chem 170 modules)
  • January 30: university closed and class canceled due to weather
  • February 1: review of chemical reactions and an introduction to thermodynamics (read: 7.2, 9.1; problems: 7.15, 17, 19, 21)
  • February 4: measuring the flow of heat in a chemical reaction (read: 9.2; problems: 9.7, 9, 11, 19, 25, 27)
  • February 6: a first look at enthalpy - heats of reaction (read: 9.3 and What is a Mole Reaction?; problems: 9.39, 41)
  • February 8: a second look at enthalpy - atom combinations and the importance of state functions (read: 9.3; problems: none)
  • February 11: a third look at enthalpy - heats of formation (read: 9.3; problems: 9.55, 59, 61, 69, 81)
  • February 13: where did all the heat go - entropy (read: 12.1-3; problems: 12.13, 15, 17, 25)
  • February 15: what makes a reaction favorable - free energy (read: 12.4; problems: 12.31, 33, 35, 37)
  • February 18: driving an unfavorable reaction (no reading assignment or suggested problems)
  • February 20: thermodynamics of redox reactions (read: 16.1-4; problems: 16.23, 29)
  • February 22: first unit exam

Unit II: Equilibrium Chemistry

In which we learn to predict the composition of a system when it reaches a state of equilibrium and apply this to systems of acid-base reactions, solubility reactions, and/or complexation reactions.

  • February 25: thermodynamics and equilbria (read: 12.4, 13.1-2; problems: 13.9, 15, 17)
  • February 27: general approaches to solving equilibrium problems (read: 13.4 and some thoughts on solving equilibrium problems; problems: 13.51, 53, 55, 65, 75, 79, 83)
  • March 1: types of equilibrium reactions and predicting what happens when we disturb a reaction at equilibrium (read: 13.3; problems: 13.31, 37, 41, 45, 47)
  • March 4: monoprotic weak acids and weak bases (read: 14.1-4 and why we ignore water's dissociation constant when solving pH problems; problems: 14.1, 7, 9, 17, 19, 25, 35, 57, 69, 71)
  • March 6: fractional abundance diagrams and polyprotic acids and bases (read: 14.5 and relative importance of a conjugate acid and its conjugate base, ladder diagrams, and polyprotic acids and bases; problems: none)
  • March 8: introduction to buffers (read: 14.6; problems: 14.87, 89, 91, 93, 97, 99)
  • March 11: how buffers work (read: calculating a buffer's pH and evaluating its effectiveness; problems: 14.101)
  • March 13: case study on characterizing a buffer's properties (read: none; problems: none)
  • March 15: case study on designing a buffer (read: three ways to prepare a buffer; problems: 14.103, 105)
  • March 18: solubility equilbria (read: 15.1; problems: 15.3, 11, 13)
  • March 20: controlling solubility using common ions and pH (read: 15.4 and finding the equilibrium constant for a new reaction; problems: 15.15, 25)
  • March 22: complexation equilibria (read: 15.2,3; problems: 15.63)
  • March 25: spring break
  • March 27: spring break
  • March 29: spring break
  • April 1: why equilibrium constants aren't constant (read: none; problems: none)
  • April 3: catch-up and review for second unit exam
  • April 5: second unit exam

Unit III: Kinetics of Chemical Reactions

In which we explore how a reaction's progress changes with time and what this macroscopic information conveys about the reaction at a microscopic level.

  • April 8: introduction to kinetics (read: 17.1-2; problems: 17.1, 3, 5)
  • April 10: rate laws for chemical reactions (read: 17.3-4; problems: 17.13, 15, 17, 19, 47)
  • April 12: studying kinetics in the lab using pseudo-order conditions (read: 17.3-4; problems: 17.33, 35)
  • April 15: studying kinetics in the lab using initial rates (read: 17.3-4; problems: 17.21, 23, 25, 31)
  • April 17: kinetics of reactions with multiple pathways (no reading assignment or problems)
  • April 19: reaction mechanisms (read: 17.6; problems: 17.69, 73, 75)
  • April 22: activation energy (read: 17.5; problems: 17.61, 63)
  • April 24: modeling the arrhenius equation (read: 17.5; problems: none)
  • April 26: thermodynamics and kinetics (no reading assignment or problems)
  • April 29: catch-up and review for third unit exam
  • May 1: third unit exam

Unit IV: Wrapping Up

In which we revisit the topics of Units I, II, and III, reviewing what we covered and extending our understanding of the relationships between thermodynamics, equilibrium chemistry, and kinetics.

  • May 3: review of thermodynamics
  • May 6: review of equilibrium chemistry
  • May 8: review of kinetics

Final Exam

  • Wednesday, Msy 15th from 8:30 - 11:30
