Chemistry 260, which is one of four courses comprising the department's introductory core, provides an introduction to physical and analytical chemistry. As its name implies, this course focuses on three topics of central importance to understanding chemical reactivity: thermodynamics, equilibria, and kinetics.

Thermodynamics is the study of the energetics of chemical reactions. Experience shows us that many reactions are not reversible; for example, in a gas furnace the combustion of methane, CH4, in the presence of oxygen, O2, produces carbon dioxide, CO2, water, H2O, and heat:

CH4 + 2O2 â€”> CO2 + 2H2O

The reverse reaction, under identical conditions, does not occur; that is, we do not expect the carbon dioxide we exhale to react with moisture in the air to produce methane and oxygen. Of particular interest to us is our ability to predict whether a chemical reaction will occur under a given set of conditions.

Once it begins, a chemical reaction continues until the reactants and the products no longer change concentration. We call the reaction's end point a state of equilibrium. Of particular interest to us is our ability to predict the composition of a system at equilibrium and to predict how a system at equilibrium responds to a change in its conditions.

Although thermodynamics allows us to predict a reaction's favorability, we cannot predict whether the reaction happens in a reasonable amount of time. For example, thermodynamics tells us that a mixture of methane, CH4, and oxygen, O2, will react to make water. Mixtures of the two gases, however, are stable indefinitely unless we first add energy, perhaps in the form of a spark. The study of kinetics helps us understand the factors that influence how quickly a reaction occurs. The study of kinetics also allows us to explain how a chemical reaction proceeds on a microscopic level.
